Intro Part:

Renewable energy, often referred to as clean energy, comes from natural sources or processes that are constantly replenished. Renewable energy is often thought of as a new technology. Harnessing nature’s power has long been used for heating, transportation, lighting, and more.

NCSEA defines clean energy as energy derived from renewable, zero-emissions sources (“renewables”), as well as energy saved through energy efficiency (“EE”) measures.

Although all the different forms of renewable energy can be used, the most efficient forms of renewable energy are geothermal, solar, wind, hydroelectricity, and biomass.

Since the 21st century, as the global climate problem is rising, the energy problem has become more and more serious. This requires us to explore new clean energy sources. The development and utilization of new energy is not only can meet the needs of the rapid economic growth nowadays, but also is the inevitable choice of sustainable development strategy in our country

Solar Energy:

Humans have been harnessing solar energy for thousands of years—to grow crops, stay warm, and dry foods. According to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory, “more energy from the sun falls on the earth in one hour than is used by everyone in the world in one year.” Today, we use the sun’s rays in many ways—to heat homes and businesses, to warm water, or power devices. Solar energy systems don’t produce air pollutants or greenhouse gases, and as long as they are responsibly sited, most solar panels have few environmental impacts beyond the manufacturing process.

Geothermal energy

If you’ve ever relaxed in a hot spring, you’ve used geothermal energy. The earth’s core is about as hot as the sun’s surface. Drilling deep wells brings very hot underground water to the surface as a hydrothermal resource, which is then pumped through a turbine to create electricity. Geothermal plants typically have low emissions if they pump the steam and water they use back into the reservoir.

Ocean

Tidal and wave energy is still in a developmental phase, but the ocean will always be ruled by the moon’s gravity, which makes harnessing its power an attractive option.

Geothermal Heat Pumps

Geothermal technology is a new take on a recognizable process—the coils at the back of your fridge are a mini heat pump, removing heat from the interior to keep foods fresh and cool.

The solar-thermal power generation

 The solar-thermal power generation is a station that converts the full spectrum of solar energy into heat energy by the absorber, then the heat radiation which is larger than the energy of the photovoltaic cell can be emitted by the wavelength selective emitter, Finally use the photovoltaic effect to generate electricity. The solar-thermal power generation combines the advantages of two forms of solar and thermal, so it is a promising way of solar energy utilization. The literature [5] proposed an all-carbon solar thermos photovoltaic (CSTPV), based on the energy balance model and equivalent circuit model, a reliable theoretical model of CSTPV system is established, under the same area ratio, the system efficiency increases with the increase of temperature and under the same temperature condition, the system efficiency increases with the increase of the area ratio.

Fuel Cells

 Fuel cells as the fourth Power generation mode after thermal power, hydropower, nuclear, known as the twenty first Century clean, efficient power source, has been widely concerned by people, and fuel cell technology is developing rapidly [7]. The fuel cell is a kind of power plant which has the chemical energy into electric energy directly, which has high power generation efficiency and less environmental pollution. According to the electrolyte, the fuel cell can be roughly divided into the alkaline fuel cell, the phosphoric acid fuel cell, the solid oxide fuel cell, the molten carbonate fuel cell and the proton exchange membrane fuel cell. The working principle of the fuel cell is the inverse process of the electrolysis water, that is, fuel and oxidant make the conversion of the electricity, water and heat through the electrode.

Climate Change:

The Paris Agreement sets a goal to limit the increase in global average temperature to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and to attempt to limit the increase to 1.5°C. Renewable Energy, coupled with energy efficiency gains, can provide 90% of the CO2 emissions reductions needed by 2050. Renewable energy is therefore a key component of Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) – the central implementation tool for countries under the Paris Agreement.

Energy Transition:

The energy transition is a pathway toward transformation of the global energy sector from fossil-based to zero-carbon by the second half of this century. According to IRENA’s latest Global Renewables Outlook 2020, around 65% of total final energy use will be satisfied by renewables in 2050, which will also account for approximately 86% of electricity generation. This includes  60% of electricity to be supplied by solar photovoltaics and wind.

Technology:

Innovations in renewable energy encompass all new approaches that help to overcome barriers and result in an accelerated deployment of renewables to support the energy transition innovation holds the key to a cost-effective global energy transformation. Energy transition requires systemic innovation, matching and leveraging synergies in innovations across all sectors and components of the system, and involving all actors. It includes innovations in information technology, policy frameworks, market design, business models, finance instruments, enabling infrastructure and sector coupling.

Can clean energy replace fossil fuels?

Yes! However, despite falling costs, wind and solar only produce a little over 5.5% of the world’s electricity. A few countries, as well as several states in the U.S., have ambitious goals for reducing their greenhouse gas emissions. Germany, for instance, aims to run on 80% renewable energy by 2050. The shift to 100% clean energy will depend on small-scale progress and cooperation, but it is doable.

The transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy will not occur overnight, and it will not escape recurring setbacks. Nevertheless, renewables are likely to replace fossil fuels as the dominant source of electrical power well before mid-century as well as make giant strides in other areas such as transportation.

The short answer is yes. Renewable energy can and will replace fossil fuels in the future, but it will take time for the world to adjust to reducing their reliance on fossil fuels.

How can we design and implement an effective energy efficiency program?

Utilities and regulators have increasingly turned to energy efficiency in recent years to provide cost-effective means of solving capacity and grid management concerns while also achieving environmental goals.

  1. Understanding the value of efficiency to utilities and ratepayers is an important first step.
  2. complete a potential study, which looks at the building stock, installed equipment, and customer population within a utility’s jurisdiction
  3. Utilities should consider the variety of cost-effective measures that they wish to incentivize, and based on the upfront cost of the measure and the long-term value of the savings they provide should establish an incentive level that is appropriate for each measure.
  4. Utilities should devise an implementation plan that is appropriate for each measure.Programs that target large HVAC equipment or commercial installations often require networks of trade allies, who work with utilities to implement the programs effectively
  5. A best practice in the efficiency sector is to conduct an annual evaluation of all energy efficiency programs

Challenges in using clean energy:

  1. Variability : The variable nature of wind and solar poses problems for balancing supply and demand on different timescales (from seconds to seasons) and on different spatial scales. The distributed nature of these energy resources also adds complexity in terms of connecting them to the existing grid infrastructure. These aspects mean that we need to amend and redesign not only the way in which we deliver electricity, but also the electricity market, which is designed for fossil fuel generators. Associated with this redesign is the need for new regulation and governance models and procedures.

The solution to variable power production is a mix of flexible generation & demand, interconnections and storage capacity.One way to avoid such power cuts in the future to ensure more battery reserve capacity: doubling this would be extremely cost-effective compared to the economic costs that would be incurred from power cuts.

  1. Energy not just electricity : A huge challenge we must overcome is how to use renewables for low cost energy, not just electricity. While renewables are playing an increasing role providing electricity; they are overshadowed by our much greater use of gas and oil for space heating and transport, see Figure 3. We urgently need to decrease  energy demand and increase energy efficiency.
  2. Changing mindsets : Arguably the biggest challenges to overcome are not the technical aspects, but making the necessary changes to policy, regulation and markets, and helping everyone in society shift to a low carbon future. The changes needed include correcting market failures, policy interventions such as carbon pricing, and changes to the way in which social and network levies are implemented.
  3. Availability of Power: One of the biggest concerns in the field of renewable energy is power generation depending on natural resources that are uncontrollable by humans.
  4. Power Quality Issues: Consistently high power quality is needed to ensure stability and high efficiency of the network. The quality of the power supply allows the system to work well with high reliability and lower costs. On the other hand, poor power quality can have major adverse effects on the power grid as well as industrial processes
  5. Resource Location: Most renewable energy plants that share their energy with the grid require large areas of space.some renewable energy sources are simply not available in different regions. the distance between the renewable energy source and the grid is a major aspect in term of cost and efficiency

For example, in exploiting solar power, how to track the maximum solar radiation point is the key to improve the efficiency of solar power generation.   13. The safety problems: Security is the first priority in the development and utilization of new energy. The incident of a Japanese nuclear power plant leakage gives us a wake-up alarm, the nuclear radiation is harmful to human health. While we make full use of nuclear energy, we should pay attention to safety monitoring, safe operation, we should strengthen the construction of safe operating systems, and we need to set up a good safety awareness. At present, the nuclear waste disposal mechanism is not perfect. The nuclear pollution treatment needs to be further explored
  14. The economic efficiency problems: New energy development can solve the problem of shortage of resources, it can improve productivity, and however, we should improve economic efficiency. The development and application of new energy resources lacks the theoretical system of global optimization. The power instability and strong randomness characteristic of the new energy power and the demand of ensuring the power supply continuously and stably are lost in sharp contradiction. In accordance with the conventional mode of development and utilization, it leads to the setting of large scale redundant backup power supply, the low efficiency of power transmission and so on, which results in low development costs, low utilization. For example, in solar power, the construction of solar energy needs a large investment, but the profit is relatively small, there is no scale effect, which is mainly due to cost and technology.
